# URP-SP14: Decision Authority Mapping

**Type:** Strategic Pattern (Tier 1)
**Function:** Clarifying decision rights in complex situations
**Domain Independence:** Universal across any multi-party decision context
**Orchestration Role:** Establishes governance foundation before substantive work

## Pattern Definition

### Trigger Condition
Situations involving:
- Multiple parties or functions with potential authority
- Unclear decision rights or overlapping jurisdictions
- Complex organizational structures
- Need for investment in substantive work
- Coordination requirements across boundaries

### Core Procedure

1. **Decision Identification**
   - What decisions need to be made?
   - What's the hierarchy of decisions?
   - What decisions are interdependent?
   - What's the decision sequence?

2. **Authority Assessment**
   - Who has formal authority for each decision?
   - Who claims authority?
   - Where does authority conflict or overlap?
   - What's the basis for each authority claim?

3. **Authority Resolution**
   - How are conflicting claims resolved?
   - What escalation paths exist?
   - Who decides when authority is disputed?
   - What precedents apply?

4. **Decision Process Design**
   - How will each decision be made?
   - Who has input vs. decision rights?
   - What information is required?
   - What timeline applies?

5. **Authority Confirmation**
   - Do all parties agree on authority mapping?
   - Is authority documented and communicated?
   - What happens if authority is challenged?
   - How is authority enforced?

### Expert Heuristic

> "Never solve the wrong problem perfectly. Before investing heavily in problem-solving, verify that you're solving for the right decision-maker. Getting the right answer to present to someone who can't decide is wasted effort."

## Evidence from Literature

### Example 1: RACI Frameworks
**Context:** Project management
**Evidence:** Clarifying Responsible/Accountable/Consulted/Informed roles prevents governance confusion
**Insight:** Decision authority requires explicit mapping

### Example 2: Organizational Decision Rights
**Context:** Strategic management (Jensen & Meckling)
**Evidence:** Clear decision rights improve organizational performance; ambiguity creates dysfunction
**Insight:** Authority clarity matters for effectiveness

### Example 3: Stakeholder Salience
**Context:** Mitchell et al. (1997) stakeholder theory
**Evidence:** Stakeholder influence on decisions depends on power, legitimacy, and urgency
**Insight:** Authority is multi-dimensional

### Example 4: Matrix Organizations
**Context:** Organizational design research
**Evidence:** Matrix structures require explicit decision authority to avoid gridlock
**Insight:** Complex structures need explicit authority mapping

## Decision Criteria

**Criterion 1: Authority Clarity**
- IF authority is clear, THEN proceed to substance
- IF authority is unclear, THEN clarify before investing
- Rationale: Substantive work without clear authority is high-risk

**Criterion 2: Conflict Resolution**
- IF authority conflicts, THEN resolve before proceeding
- IF escalation needed, THEN escalate early
- Rationale: Unresolved authority conflicts will resurface

**Criterion 3: Process Fit**
- IF decision process matches authority, THEN governance is aligned
- IF process bypasses authority, THEN expect problems
- Rationale: Process must respect authority structure

**Criterion 4: Investment Calibration**
- IF authority is confirmed, THEN full investment justified
- IF authority is provisional, THEN limit investment until confirmed
- Rationale: Match investment to authority certainty

## Contrast with Naive Approaches

**Naive Approach**: Assume authority; find out later you're presenting to wrong person.
**Expert Approach**: Map authority first; confirm before major investment.

**Naive Approach**: Treat all stakeholders as equal decision-makers.
**Expert Approach**: Differentiate decision-makers from influencers from consultees.

**Naive Approach**: Work within ambiguity; hope it resolves.
**Expert Approach**: Surface and resolve authority questions explicitly.

**Naive Approach**: Focus on getting right answer; assume decision will follow.
**Expert Approach**: Ensure answer goes to someone who can decide.
