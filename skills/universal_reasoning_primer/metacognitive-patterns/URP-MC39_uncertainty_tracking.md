# URP-MC39: Uncertainty Tracking

**Type:** Meta-Cognitive Pattern (Tier 3)
**Function:** Maintaining explicit awareness of knowledge uncertainty
**Domain Independence:** Universal across all reasoning and decision-making
**Orchestration Role:** Ensures decisions account for uncertainty; prevents false confidence

## Pattern Definition

### Trigger Condition
Situations involving:
- Making decisions under incomplete information
- Drawing conclusions from limited evidence
- Communicating knowledge to others
- Planning where outcomes are uncertain
- Integrating information of varying reliability

### Core Procedure

1. **Uncertainty Identification**
   - What do I know with high confidence?
   - What do I believe but with uncertainty?
   - What are the key unknowns?
   - What could I be wrong about?

2. **Uncertainty Characterization**
   - Is this aleatory uncertainty (inherent randomness)?
   - Is this epistemic uncertainty (knowledge gap)?
   - Can this uncertainty be reduced?
   - How might this uncertainty resolve?

3. **Uncertainty Quantification**
   - How confident am I (probability estimate)?
   - What's the range of possibilities?
   - What would change my estimate?
   - How would I bet on this?

4. **Uncertainty Propagation**
   - How does uncertainty in inputs affect conclusions?
   - Which uncertainties matter most?
   - Are uncertainties compounding?
   - What's the overall uncertainty of conclusions?

5. **Uncertainty Communication**
   - How do I convey uncertainty appropriately?
   - Am I being clear about what's known vs. uncertain?
   - Am I avoiding false precision?
   - Am I appropriately hedged?

### Expert Heuristic

> "Knowing what you don't know is more important than knowing what you know. False certainty is more dangerous than acknowledged ignorance. The expert carries an uncertainty map alongside their knowledge map."

## Evidence from Literature

### Example 1: Calibration Research
**Context:** Lichtenstein et al. (1982) confidence calibration
**Evidence:** People often fail to track uncertainty accurately; can be improved
**Insight:** Uncertainty tracking is a skill that can be developed

### Example 2: Probabilistic Reasoning
**Context:** Kahneman & Tversky decision research
**Evidence:** People struggle with probability; explicit uncertainty thinking helps
**Insight:** Making uncertainty explicit improves reasoning

### Example 3: Expert Judgment
**Context:** Tetlock (2005) forecasting research
**Evidence:** Best forecasters are explicit about uncertainty and update it
**Insight:** Good forecasters track and communicate uncertainty

## Decision Criteria

**Criterion 1: Decision Weighting**
- IF uncertainty is high, THEN weight conclusion less in decisions
- IF uncertainty is low, THEN can rely more heavily
- Rationale: Decisions should account for uncertainty

**Criterion 2: Action Threshold**
- IF uncertainty too high for stakes, THEN gather more information
- IF uncertainty acceptable for stakes, THEN can proceed
- Rationale: Stakes determine acceptable uncertainty

**Criterion 3: Communication Requirement**
- IF communicating conclusions, THEN include uncertainty
- IF precision implied but not warranted, THEN add hedging
- Rationale: Responsible communication includes uncertainty

**Criterion 4: Reduction Priority**
- IF uncertainty is reducible and matters, THEN invest in reduction
- IF uncertainty is irreducible or low-stakes, THEN accept
- Rationale: Not all uncertainty is worth reducing

## Contrast with Naive Approaches

**Naive Approach**: State conclusions without uncertainty qualification.
**Expert Approach**: Explicitly track and communicate uncertainty levels.

**Naive Approach**: Treat all beliefs as equally certain.
**Expert Approach**: Differentiate high-confidence from low-confidence beliefs.

**Naive Approach**: False precision; "it will take 3.5 days."
**Expert Approach**: Appropriate precision; "probably 2-5 days."

**Naive Approach**: Uncertainty is weakness to hide.
**Expert Approach**: Appropriate uncertainty is epistemic virtue.
