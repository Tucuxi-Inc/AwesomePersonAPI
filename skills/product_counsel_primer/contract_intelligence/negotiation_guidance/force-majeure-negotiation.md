## Post-COVID Negotiation Landscape

### What Changed

**Pre-COVID:**
- Force majeure clauses often boilerplate
- Pandemic rarely listed explicitly
- Supply chain disruption underappreciated
- "Reasonable efforts" often undefined

**Post-COVID:**
- Pandemic explicitly addressed
- Supply chain resilience expected
- Business continuity planning scrutinized
- Remote work capabilities assumed
- Notice requirements more specific

## Event Definition Negotiations

### Broad Definition (Obligor-Favorable)

```
FORCE MAJEURE EVENTS

"Force Majeure Event" means any event beyond the reasonable
control of the affected party, including without limitation:

(a) Acts of God, natural disasters, earthquakes, floods, fires;
(b) War, terrorism, civil unrest, armed conflict;
(c) Epidemic, pandemic, quarantine, public health emergency;
(d) Government action, law, regulation, embargo, sanction;
(e) Labor disputes, strikes, lockouts;
(f) Utility failures, power outages, telecommunications failure;
(g) Supply chain disruption, shortage of materials;
(h) Cyberattack, system intrusion, ransomware;
(i) Any other event beyond reasonable control.

The catch-all provision (i) shall be interpreted broadly.
```

### Narrow Definition (Obligee-Favorable)

```
FORCE MAJEURE EVENTS

"Force Majeure Event" means only the following events to the
extent beyond the reasonable control of the affected party:

(a) War, terrorism, or armed conflict;
(b) Natural disaster directly affecting performance location;
(c) Government-mandated closure or quarantine.

The following are expressly NOT Force Majeure Events:
- Economic conditions, currency fluctuations
- Labor disputes (party should have labor planning)
- Supply chain issues (party should have redundancy)
- Cyberattacks (party should have security)
- Pandemic (party should have continuity plans)
- Foreseeable events at time of contracting
```

### Balanced Approach

```
FORCE MAJEURE EVENTS

"Force Majeure Event" means: acts of God, war, terrorism,
government action preventing performance, natural disaster,
or pandemic, in each case:
(a) Beyond the reasonable control of the affected party;
(b) Not reasonably foreseeable at the Effective Date;
(c) Not caused or contributed to by the affected party;
(d) The effects of which could not be avoided through
    reasonable business continuity measures.

Specifically excluded: economic conditions, labor availability,
cost increases, and supply chain issues unless caused by an
enumerated event affecting the supplier.
```

## Pandemic-Specific Provisions

### Pandemic Carve-Out

```
PANDEMIC PROVISIONS

(a) KNOWN RISK. The parties acknowledge they are entering this
    Agreement during [COVID-19 / ongoing pandemic awareness].
    Future waves, variants, or similar pandemic events shall
    not constitute Force Majeure unless they result in:
    - Government-mandated closure of performance location; or
    - Travel restrictions preventing essential personnel access.

(b) CONTINUITY EXPECTATION. Each party represents it has
    business continuity capabilities for remote operations.
    Inability to work remotely is not Force Majeure.

(c) SUPPLY CHAIN. Supply chain disruption is not Force Majeure
    unless both: (i) caused by pandemic; and (ii) party had
    diversified supply chain and still could not obtain.
```

### Pandemic as Force Majeure

```
PANDEMIC FORCE MAJEURE

Pandemic, epidemic, or public health emergency shall constitute
Force Majeure only to the extent causing:
(a) Government-mandated closure exceeding [30] consecutive days;
(b) Quarantine of essential personnel exceeding [14] days;
(c) Travel restrictions preventing performance for [30]+ days;
(d) Hospitalization or death of key personnel.

The affected party must demonstrate specific impact, not merely
general pandemic conditions.
```

## Notice and Mitigation Requirements

### Customer-Favorable (Strict Requirements)

```
FORCE MAJEURE NOTICE

The affected party shall:

(a) PROMPT NOTICE. Notify the other party within [24] hours
    of becoming aware of a Force Majeure Event, including:
    - Description of the event
    - Specific obligations affected
    - Expected duration
    - Mitigation steps being taken

(b) ONGOING UPDATES. Provide weekly updates on status and
    expected resumption date.

(c) MITIGATION. Use commercially reasonable efforts to:
    - Continue performance through alternative means
    - Minimize duration and impact
    - Prioritize the other party's work when resuming

(d) DOCUMENTATION. Upon request, provide documentation
    supporting the Force Majeure claim.

Failure to comply with notice requirements waives Force
Majeure defense for the period of non-compliance.
```

### Vendor-Favorable (Flexible Requirements)

```
NOTICE

The affected party shall provide reasonable notice of Force
Majeure conditions as soon as practicable. Late notice does
not waive Force Majeure rights if the event qualifies.
```

## Allocation of Risk During Force Majeure

### Payment Obligations

**Customer Position (Suspend Payment):**
```
During Force Majeure affecting Vendor's performance:
(a) Customer's payment obligations are suspended;
(b) Customer shall receive pro-rata refund for prepaid
    Services not delivered;
(c) Customer is not obligated to pay for partial performance
    unless it elects to accept such performance.
```

**Vendor Position (Continue Payment):**
```
Force Majeure does not suspend Customer's payment obligations
for: (a) Services rendered before the event; (b) license fees
and subscription fees (which continue regardless of use);
(c) costs incurred by Vendor in attempting performance.
```

**Balanced Approach:**
```
PAYMENT DURING FORCE MAJEURE

(a) License/subscription fees: Continue (access remains available)
(b) Usage-based fees: Suspended during non-availability
(c) Services fees: Suspended for undelivered services
(d) Prepaid amounts: Pro-rata credit for extended outages
```

### Continued Obligations

```
UNAFFECTED OBLIGATIONS

Force Majeure does not excuse:
(a) Payment of amounts accrued before the event;
(b) Confidentiality and data protection obligations;
(c) Indemnification for claims arising before the event;
(d) Obligations that by their nature can be performed remotely;
(e) Security of Customer Data in Vendor's possession.
```

## Termination Rights

### Customer-Favorable (Early Termination)

```
TERMINATION FOR EXTENDED FORCE MAJEURE

If Force Majeure prevents Vendor's performance for:
(a) [30] consecutive days: Customer may terminate affected
    Services without penalty and receive pro-rata refund;
(b) [60] consecutive days: Customer may terminate entire
    Agreement and receive full refund of prepaid amounts;
(c) [90] days in any [12] month period: Customer may terminate
    as above.

Customer's termination right survives for [30] days after
Force Majeure ends.
```

### Vendor-Favorable (Mutual/Extended)

```
TERMINATION

If Force Majeure prevents performance for [180] consecutive
days, either party may terminate without liability. Termination
requires [30] days notice during which the parties shall
attempt to agree on modified performance.

Neither party is entitled to damages for Force Majeure
termination.
```

### Balanced Termination

```
TERMINATION FOR FORCE MAJEURE

(a) Either party may terminate if Force Majeure continues for
    [90] consecutive days or [120] days in any [12] months.

(b) The non-affected party may terminate earlier ([60] days)
    if: (i) the event is reasonably expected to continue; and
    (ii) continued delay would materially harm the non-affected
    party's business.

(c) Upon termination, the affected party shall: (i) refund
    prepaid amounts for undelivered Services; (ii) transition
    to successor as reasonably requested.
```

### By Industry

| **Events/Hospitality** | Government closures, travel restrictions |
| **Construction** | Weather, material shortages, labor |
| **Financial Services** | Market disruptions, regulatory action |
| **Healthcare** | Pandemic, regulatory, staffing |

### Supply Chain Provisions

```
SUPPLY CHAIN FORCE MAJEURE

Supply chain disruption is Force Majeure only if Vendor:
(a) Maintained diversified supply sources for critical components;
(b) Maintained reasonable safety stock inventory;
(c) Could not obtain alternative supply despite diligent efforts;
(d) Affected supplier itself suffered Force Majeure event.

Vendor shall notify Customer within [5] days of becoming aware
of supply issues that may affect delivery.
```

## Leverage-Based Negotiation (BI2)

### High Customer Leverage

**Customer Can Demand:**
- Narrow Force Majeure definition
- Exclusion of foreseeable events
- Strict notice requirements
- Short termination triggers (30-60 days)
- Full refund rights
- No payment during Force Majeure

### High Vendor Leverage

**Vendor Can Maintain:**
- Broad Force Majeure definition
- Including pandemics and cyber events
- Flexible notice requirements
- Long termination triggers (180+ days)
- Continued payment for licenses
- Limitation on refunds

## Negotiation Scripts

### Customer Narrowing Force Majeure

> "Given lessons learned from recent events, we need a Force
> Majeure clause that accounts for modern business realities.
> Pandemic, cyber events, and supply chain issues are now
> foreseeable risks that vendors should plan for through
> business continuity and diversification. We'll accept Force
> Majeure for truly uncontrollable events, but with strict
> notice requirements and reasonable termination triggers."

### Vendor Seeking Broader Protection

> "Force Majeure should protect both parties from events truly
> beyond control. While we have robust continuity plans, we
> can't guarantee performance through every possible scenario.
> We're comfortable with reasonable notice requirements and
> mitigation obligations, but need protection for significant
> disruptions that affect our ability to deliver."

### Resolution Approach

> "Let's agree that traditional Force Majeure events - war,
> natural disaster, government action - are covered. For
> pandemic and cyber events, we'll include them if they
> result in specific measurable impacts like mandated closure
> or system unavailability. You'll provide 48-hour notice with
> weekly updates, and after 60 days we can both terminate."

## Decision Matrix

| Event Type | Include as FM? | Conditions |
|------------|---------------|------------|
| War/terrorism | Yes | Beyond control |
| Natural disaster | Yes | Directly affecting performance |
| Government closure | Yes | Mandatory, not voluntary |
| Pandemic (general) | Narrow | Specific impact required |
| Supply chain | Narrow | Despite diversification |
| Cyberattack | Depends | Despite security measures |
| Labor disputes | Usually no | Should plan for |
| Economic conditions | No | Commercial risk |
