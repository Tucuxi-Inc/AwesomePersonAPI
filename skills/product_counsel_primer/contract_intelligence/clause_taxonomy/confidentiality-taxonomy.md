# Confidentiality Clause Taxonomy

## Overview

Confidentiality provisions protect non-public information exchanged between parties during business relationships. They appear in standalone NDAs (Non-Disclosure Agreements) and as sections within larger commercial agreements. This taxonomy catalogs the key variations and decision points observed across 3,000+ contract samples.

## Structure Types

### 1. Mutual vs. Unilateral

**Mutual NDA (MNDA)**
Both parties disclose and receive confidential information; obligations are reciprocal.
- Most common in: Due diligence, technology evaluations, partnerships
- Key language: "Each party may disclose... the Receiving Party shall..."

**Unilateral NDA**
Only one party discloses; obligations run one direction.
- Most common in: Vendor evaluations, employment contexts, investor pitches
- Key language: "Discloser shall provide... Recipient agrees to..."

### 2. Standalone vs. Embedded

**Standalone NDA**
Separate agreement focused solely on confidentiality.
- Typically executed early in relationship before substantive deal terms
- Often includes "business relationship" purpose clause

**Embedded Confidentiality Section**
Confidentiality provisions within larger agreements (MSA, License, Services).
- May reference or supersede prior NDAs
- Scope often tied to specific transaction

## Core Components

### A. Definition of Confidential Information

**Marking-Based Definition**
Requires information to be marked or designated as confidential.

```
"Confidential Information" means information disclosed in writing
and marked "Confidential" or, if disclosed orally, identified as
confidential at the time of disclosure and confirmed in writing
within [30] days.
```
- **Vendor-favorable**: Reduces scope; information easily excluded
- **Risk**: Inadvertent disclosure of unmarked sensitive info not protected

**Broad/Reasonable Person Definition**
Information that should reasonably be considered confidential.

```
"Confidential Information" means all nonpublic information that
is designated as confidential or that, given the nature of the
information or the circumstances surrounding its disclosure,
reasonably should be considered as confidential.
```
- **Discloser-favorable**: Broader protection without marking burden
- **Common in**: Technology deals, M&A contexts

**Enumerated Categories**
Lists specific types of protected information.

```
Confidential Information includes, without limitation:
(i) technical data, trade secrets, and know-how;
(ii) research and development information;
(iii) product plans and roadmaps;
(iv) customer and supplier lists;
(v) financial information and projections;
(vi) business strategies and marketing plans.
```
- **Balanced**: Clear scope but potentially under-inclusive
- **Best practice**: Combine enumeration with catch-all provision

### B. Standard Exclusions

All well-drafted confidentiality provisions exclude:

1. **Public Domain**: Information that is or becomes publicly available without breach
2. **Prior Knowledge**: Information known to Receiving Party before disclosure
3. **Independent Development**: Information independently developed without reference to Confidential Information
4. **Third-Party Receipt**: Information received from a third party without restriction

**Variation: Documentation Requirements**
```
"...can be shown by documentation to have been known to the
Receiving Party at the time of its receipt from the Disclosing Party"
```
- **Discloser-favorable**: Requires written proof; harder for recipient to invoke
- **Recipient-favorable**: "Competent evidence" (lower bar than documentation)

### C. Permitted Disclosures

**Need-to-Know Basis**
```
The Receiving Party will restrict disclosure to its employees and
contractors who (i) have a need to know in connection with the
parties' business relationship, and (ii) are bound by written
confidentiality obligations no less restrictive than this Agreement.
```

**Variations in Scope**:
- Employees only (most restrictive)
- Employees and contractors
- Employees, contractors, and professional advisors (attorneys, accountants)
- Affiliates (parent, subsidiary, commonly-controlled entities)

**Compelled Disclosure**
Legal process requiring disclosure (subpoena, court order, regulatory inquiry).

**Standard Elements**:
1. Prompt notice to Disclosing Party (where legally permitted)
2. Reasonable cooperation in seeking protective order
3. Disclosure limited to what is legally required
4. Reasonable efforts to maintain confidential treatment

### D. Obligations and Standards of Care

**Reasonable Care Standard**
```
The Receiving Party will take reasonable measures to protect
Confidential Information, including at a minimum those measures
it takes to protect its own confidential information of a similar nature.
```

**Variations**:
- "Reasonable care" (market standard)
- "Same degree of care as own information" (ties to internal practices)
- "Best efforts" (higher standard, avoid)
- "Commercially reasonable efforts" (balanced)

### E. Term and Survival

**Fixed Term Survival**
```
This Agreement will terminate [3] years from the Effective Date;
provided that obligations regarding Confidential Information will
survive for [5] years following such termination.
```

**Common Survival Periods**:
- 2-3 years: Standard commercial deals
- 5 years: Technology/IP-heavy transactions
- 7-10 years: Highly sensitive information (trade secrets)
- Perpetual: Trade secrets that remain confidential indefinitely

**Trade Secrets Carve-Out**
```
Notwithstanding the foregoing, obligations with respect to trade
secrets shall continue for so long as such information constitutes
a trade secret under applicable law.
```

### F. Return/Destruction of Materials

**Standard Provision**
```
Upon termination or written request, the Receiving Party will
return or destroy all Confidential Information and provide
written certification of compliance.
```

**Variations**:
- Return only (Discloser preference)
- Destroy only (Recipient preference for electronic data)
- Return or destroy at Recipient's election
- Exception for legally-required retention (compliance archives)
- Exception for backup systems (automatic deletion per retention policy)

### G. Injunctive Relief

**Purpose**: Acknowledges that monetary damages may be inadequate.

```
The Receiving Party acknowledges that disclosure in violation of
this Agreement could cause irreparable harm for which monetary
damages may be inadequate. The Disclosing Party shall be entitled
to seek injunctive relief without bond.
```

**"Without bond" variation**: Waives requirement for security deposit when seeking injunction.

### H. Residuals Clause

**Recipient-Favorable Provision**
```
Either party may use the residuals resulting from access to the
other party's Confidential Information for any purpose. "Residuals"
means information in intangible form retained in the unaided memory
of persons who have had access to Confidential Information, including
ideas, concepts, know-how or techniques.
```

**Considerations**:
- Allows "head knowledge" to be used without restriction
- Does not permit copying documents or deliberate memorization
- Common in technology evaluation/partnership NDAs
- **Discloser Risk**: Dilutes trade secret protection

### I. Independent Development

```
Nothing in this Agreement shall prevent the Receiving Party from
developing products, concepts, or techniques similar to or competing
with those embodied in the Confidential Information, provided the
Receiving Party does not violate its obligations hereunder.
```

**Purpose**: Protects recipient from contamination claims.

## Special Provisions

### Non-Solicitation of Employees

Often paired with confidentiality in NDAs:
```
During the term and for [12] months thereafter, neither party
shall solicit for employment any employee of the other party
who was directly involved in the business relationship.
```

### No License Grant

Clarifies IP ownership is not transferred:
```
The disclosure of Confidential Information does not constitute
an express or implied grant of rights under any patents,
copyrights, trademarks, or other intellectual property rights.
```

### No Obligation to Proceed

Preserves freedom to walk away from negotiations:
```
Neither party is obligated to enter into any further agreement
as a result of this Agreement or any disclosures hereunder.
```

## Clause by Contract Type

| Contract Type | Confidentiality Features |
|---------------|-------------------------|
| **Standalone NDA** | Comprehensive standalone; mutual or unilateral; broad definition |
| **Software License** | Embedded; covers source code, documentation; often perpetual for source |
| **SaaS Agreement** | Customer data provisions; data processing terms; security standards |
| **Services Agreement** | Covers deliverables, work product; overlaps with IP ownership |
| **M&A/Due Diligence** | Broad scope; standstill provisions; non-solicitation |
| **Employment** | One-way; often perpetual; ties to non-compete |
| **Distribution** | Protects pricing, customer lists; territorial restrictions |

## Key Decision Points

1. **Mutual vs. Unilateral**: Who is disclosing sensitive information?
2. **Marking Requirement**: Is the burden of marking practical?
3. **Scope of Recipients**: Employees only, or contractors/advisors/affiliates?
4. **Survival Period**: How long should protection last?
5. **Trade Secrets Carve-Out**: Perpetual protection for qualifying info?
6. **Residuals Clause**: Acceptable risk for the discloser?
7. **Return vs. Destruction**: Which is more practical for electronic data?

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Over-broad definitions** that are impractical to comply with
2. **Marking requirements** that lead to inadvertent disclosure of unmarked info
3. **Inadequate exclusions** that create uncertainty
4. **Unrealistic survival periods** (perpetual obligations for non-trade-secret info)
5. **Missing compelled disclosure** provisions (recipient trapped between obligations)
6. **Residuals clauses** that undermine trade secret protection
7. **No return/destruction exception** for compliance archives
