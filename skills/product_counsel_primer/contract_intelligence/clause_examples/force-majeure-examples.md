# Force Majeure Clause Examples

## Standard Force Majeure

### Broad Definition (Excused Party Favorable)

**Example 1 - Comprehensive Force Majeure**
```
FORCE MAJEURE

Neither party shall be liable for delays in performance or non-performance
in whole or in part, or for loss, injury, delay, expenses, damages or
other casualty suffered or incurred on account of or due to, any causes
that are beyond its reasonable control, such as, without limiting the
generality of the foregoing:

- acts of God
- fires
- strikes
- trade disputes
- riots
- embargoes
- earthquakes
- storms
- acts of the government
- power losses or shortages
- inability to obtain parts or supplies
- epidemics, pandemics, or public health emergencies
- acts of terrorism or war
- civil unrest or insurrection
- Internet or telecommunications failures not caused by the party

The affected party shall give prompt notice of the force majeure event
and shall use reasonable efforts to mitigate its effects.
```

---

### Narrow Definition (Obligee Favorable)

**Example 2 - Limited Force Majeure**
```
FORCE MAJEURE

Neither party shall be liable for failure to perform its obligations
(other than payment obligations) to the extent such failure is caused
directly by events beyond that party's reasonable control that could
not have been prevented by reasonable precautions.

Force majeure events are limited to: war, terrorism, government actions
prohibiting performance, and natural disasters causing physical
destruction of facilities.

Force majeure does not include: (a) economic hardship; (b) changes in
market conditions; (c) labor disputes affecting only the affected party;
(d) failure of third-party suppliers or subcontractors; (e) inability
to obtain materials at favorable prices; (f) events that were foreseeable
at contract execution.

Payment obligations are not excused by force majeure.
```

## Pandemic-Specific Provisions

**Example 3 - Post-COVID Pandemic Clause**
```
PANDEMIC PROVISIONS

The parties acknowledge that COVID-19 and similar future public health
emergencies may affect performance. Accordingly:

(a) KNOWN CONDITIONS. The existence of COVID-19 as of the Effective Date
    shall not constitute force majeure. Force majeure shall apply only
    to new government orders, facility closures, or materially worsened
    conditions arising after the Effective Date.

(b) MITIGATION. The affected party shall implement commercially reasonable
    business continuity measures, including remote work where feasible.

(c) NOTICE. The affected party shall provide notice within [5] business
    days of any material impact and shall provide regular updates.

(d) ALTERNATIVE PERFORMANCE. The parties shall negotiate in good faith
    to identify alternative means of performance, adjusted timelines,
    or modified deliverables.

(e) EXTENDED DURATION. If force majeure continues for more than [90] days,
    either party may terminate the affected SOW without liability.
```

## Notice and Mitigation

**Example 4 - Detailed Procedural Requirements**
```
FORCE MAJEURE PROCEDURES

(a) NOTICE. The affected party shall provide written notice within
    [72 hours] of becoming aware of a force majeure event, including:
    (i) the nature of the event; (ii) expected duration; (iii) affected
    obligations; (iv) mitigation steps being taken.

(b) UPDATES. The affected party shall provide weekly status updates
    during the continuance of the force majeure event.

(c) MITIGATION. The affected party shall use commercially reasonable
    efforts to mitigate the impact, including utilizing backup systems,
    alternative suppliers, and remote capabilities.

(d) RESUMPTION. Upon cessation of the force majeure event, the affected
    party shall promptly resume performance and notify the other party.

Failure to provide timely notice or to undertake reasonable mitigation
may result in loss of force majeure protection.
```

## Termination Rights

**Example 5 - Extended Force Majeure Termination**
```
TERMINATION FOR EXTENDED FORCE MAJEURE

If a force majeure event prevents material performance for more than
[60] consecutive days or [90] days in aggregate during any [12] month
period, either party may terminate this Agreement or the affected
SOW upon [30] days written notice.

Upon such termination:
(a) Customer shall pay for Services rendered and accepted prior to
    the force majeure event;
(b) Vendor shall refund any pre-paid fees for Services not rendered;
(c) Neither party shall have further liability to the other arising
    from the termination.
```

## Supply Chain Force Majeure

**Example 6 - Supplier-Specific Provisions**
```
SUPPLY CHAIN DISRUPTIONS

(a) SUPPLIER FAILURE. Vendor's inability to obtain materials or
    components from third-party suppliers shall constitute force
    majeure only if: (i) the shortage is industry-wide; (ii) Vendor
    made reasonable efforts to secure alternative sources; and
    (iii) the shortage was not foreseeable at contract execution.

(b) ALLOCATION. During a supply shortage, Vendor shall allocate
    available supply among customers on a fair and equitable basis.

(c) PRICE ADJUSTMENTS. If material costs increase by more than [15%]
    due to force majeure, the parties shall negotiate in good faith
    regarding price adjustments.

(d) LEAD TIME EXTENSIONS. Delivery schedules shall be extended by
    a period equal to the duration of the force majeure event plus
    reasonable time to resume normal production.
```

## What Is NOT Force Majeure

**Example 7 - Explicit Exclusions**
```
EXCLUSIONS FROM FORCE MAJEURE

Force majeure shall not include:
(a) a party's financial inability to perform;
(b) changes in market conditions or prices;
(c) loss of customers or business downturns;
(d) failure to obtain necessary permits due to party's actions;
(e) events affecting only the party's subcontractors if alternative
    subcontractors are available;
(f) any event that the party could have prevented through reasonable
    precautions or business continuity planning;
(g) strikes or labor disputes limited to the affected party's workforce;
(h) IT system failures not caused by external events.

The burden of proving force majeure rests with the party claiming it.
```
