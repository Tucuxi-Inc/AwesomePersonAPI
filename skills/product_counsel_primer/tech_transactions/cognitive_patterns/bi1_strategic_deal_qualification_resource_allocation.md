# BI1: Strategic Deal Qualification & Resource Allocation

**Type:** Business Intelligence - Deal Strategy
**Focus Area:** Go/no-go decisions and resource prioritization
**Complexity:** 8/10
**Uniqueness:** 9/10

## Description
Systematically evaluate whether a transaction justifies the investment of time, negotiation capital, and organizational resources by assessing strategic value, probability of success, and opportunity costs.

## Keywords
- deal qualification
- go/no-go decision
- resource allocation
- opportunity cost
- strategic value
- negotiation ROI
- transaction prioritization

## Application Guidance

Apply strategic deal qualification by asking:

- Does this transaction create meaningful strategic value for the business?
- What is the realistic probability of successfully closing this deal?
- What resources (time, legal fees, negotiation capital) will this require?
- What alternative opportunities would we forgo by pursuing this?
- Does the expected value justify the investment?
- Are there structural reasons this deal is likely to fail regardless of our efforts?

This pattern prevents wasting resources on low-value or low-probability transactions, enabling focus on deals that truly matter.

## When to Apply
- Before committing significant resources to any transaction
- When multiple deal opportunities compete for limited resources
- When a transaction appears complex or protracted
- Before escalating negotiation efforts on a difficult deal
- When re-evaluating an ongoing negotiation that has stalled

## 1. Detailed Pattern Description

### What This Pattern Is and Why It Matters

Strategic Deal Qualification is the critical business intelligence skill of determining which transactions deserve investment of scarce resources—particularly time, money, and negotiation capital. Unlike pure legal analysis that focuses on risk identification and mitigation, this pattern asks the fundamental question: **Should we even be doing this deal?**

In business transactions, this pattern is particularly critical because:

**Resource Scarcity**: Organizations have limited bandwidth for complex negotiations. Legal teams, business development personnel, and executive attention are finite resources. Every hour spent on a marginal deal is an hour not spent on a high-value opportunity.

**Negotiation Capital**: Every battle fought depletes negotiation capital. If you push hard on ten minor points, you'll have less leverage on the critical issues. If you fight aggressively on a low-value deal, you may damage a relationship needed for a strategic partnership.

**Opportunity Cost**: Pursuing mediocre deals prevents pursuit of excellent ones. A company that commits to a 6-month negotiation with a mid-sized customer may miss the window to close a strategic partnership with a market leader.

**Probability-Adjusted Value**: A potentially valuable deal that has a 10% chance of closing may be worth less than a moderately valuable deal with an 80% probability. Expected value thinking—standard in business—must be applied to legal transactions.

**Structural Barriers**: Some deals face insurmountable obstacles: misaligned economic incentives, regulatory barriers, cultural incompatibility, or irreconcilable positions. Recognizing these early prevents wasted effort.

**Strategic Fit**: Not all revenue is good revenue. A deal may be lucrative but strategically misaligned—taking resources away from core business, creating dependencies on problematic partners, or generating precedents that constrain future deals.

### Theoretical Foundations

This pattern draws from several business frameworks:

**Real Options Theory (Black-Scholes)**: Every transaction decision is an option with a value, strike price (cost to pursue), and time to expiration. The value of pursuing a deal depends on volatility (uncertainty), time horizon, and opportunity cost of capital.

**Portfolio Theory (Markowitz)**: Organizations should manage a portfolio of transactions, balancing high-risk/high-reward opportunities with stable, lower-value deals. Concentration risk applies to business relationships as it does to investments.

**Expected Value Analysis**: The value of a deal is not its potential upside but its probability-adjusted expected value: EV = (Probability of Success × Value if Successful) - (Probability of Failure × Cost if Failed) - (Certain Costs to Pursue).

**Opportunity Cost Economics**: The true cost of any decision includes what you give up. Pursuing Deal A means not pursuing Deal B, and the difference in value between them is a real cost.

**Strategic Fit Theory (Porter)**: Transactions should align with core competencies and strategic positioning. Deals that distract from strategic focus or create misaligned dependencies destroy value even if individually profitable.

### How It Differs from Traditional Legal Analysis

**Traditional Legal Approach**: "How do we structure and negotiate this transaction to minimize risk and protect our client's interests?"

**Business Intelligence Approach**: "Should we pursue this transaction at all, and if so, how much should we invest in fighting for better terms?"

**Traditional**: Assumes the deal is worth doing and focuses on execution.
**BI1**: Questions whether the deal merits pursuit before investing heavily.

**Traditional**: Measures success as "closed with acceptable terms."
**BI1**: Measures success as "optimal allocation of resources across all opportunities."

**Traditional**: Fights for the best possible terms in each transaction.
**BI1**: Calibrates effort to strategic importance, accepting worse terms on low-priority deals to preserve capital for critical battles.

### Relationship to Other Patterns

**BI1 + S1 (Situation Framing)**: S1 identifies stakeholders and their interests; BI1 evaluates whether those interests can be aligned to make a deal viable.

**BI1 + S4 (Risk Assessment)**: S4 identifies risks; BI1 determines whether those risks are acceptable given strategic value, or whether they make the deal non-viable.

**BI1 + S8 (Scenario Planning)**: S8 generates scenarios; BI1 uses probability-adjusted outcomes to calculate expected value.

**BI1 + BI2 (Economic Incentive Design)**: BI1 determines if a deal is worth pursuing; BI2 designs structures to make it work if it is.

## 2. Step-by-Step Framework

### Phase 1: Strategic Value Assessment

**Step 1.1: Quantify Direct Economic Value**

Calculate the tangible financial impact of the transaction.

Questions to ask:
- What is the revenue or cost savings over the contract term?
- What are the margins on this revenue?
- What is the lifetime value of this relationship?
- What are the direct costs (legal fees, implementation, ongoing servicing)?
- What is the net present value (NPV) of the cash flows?

**Example Calculation**:
- Annual contract value: $500K
- Contract term: 3 years
- Estimated gross margin: 60%
- Total gross profit: $900K
- Legal + negotiation costs: $100K
- Implementation costs: $150K
- **Net value: $650K**

**Step 1.2: Assess Strategic Value**

Evaluate non-financial strategic benefits.

Strategic value drivers:
- **Market Access**: Does this deal open access to a new market, customer segment, or geography?
- **Competitive Positioning**: Does this strengthen our position relative to competitors? Block a competitor?
- **Technology Access**: Do we gain access to critical technology, data, or capabilities?
- **Reference Value**: Is this a prestigious customer/partner that will attract others?
- **Learning Value**: Will this deal generate insights applicable to future transactions?
- **Option Value**: Does this create options for future business (upsell, expansion, partnership deepening)?
- **Ecosystem Effects**: Does this strengthen our broader ecosystem or platform?

**Scoring Framework**:
- High strategic value: 2x-5x direct economic value
- Moderate strategic value: 1.5x-2x direct economic value
- Low strategic value: 1x-1.25x direct economic value
- Negative strategic value: <1x (deal is strategically harmful)

**Step 1.3: Calculate Total Value**

Total Deal Value = Direct Economic Value × Strategic Multiplier

**Example**:
- Direct economic value: $650K
- Strategic assessment: High - leading enterprise customer in target vertical, will serve as reference for 10+ similar prospects
- Strategic multiplier: 3x
- **Total deal value: $1.95M**

### Phase 2: Probability of Success Assessment

**Step 2.1: Evaluate Structural Feasibility**

Identify fundamental barriers that might make the deal impossible.

Structural barriers:
- **Economic Alignment**: Do both parties have positive expected value? Or is this zero-sum?
- **Regulatory Barriers**: Are there legal or regulatory obstacles that might be insurmountable?
- **Technical Feasibility**: Are there technical integration or capability gaps that cannot be bridged?
- **Cultural Compatibility**: Are organizational cultures or business models fundamentally incompatible?
- **Timing Alignment**: Do both parties need this deal on compatible timelines?
- **Authority and Budget**: Does the counterparty have actual authority and budget to complete the transaction?

**Red Flags (Structural Problems)**:
- Counterparty lacks budget or authority
- Deal requires regulatory approval with <30% historical success rate
- Economic model doesn't work for one party without unrealistic assumptions
- Technical integration requires capabilities neither party possesses
- Timeline mismatch (one party needs immediate close, other needs 6-month evaluation)

**Step 2.2: Assess Negotiation Complexity**

Evaluate how difficult it will be to reach agreement.

Complexity factors:
- **Number of Contentious Issues**: How many material points of disagreement exist?
- **Positions Gap**: How far apart are the parties on key terms?
- **Internal Alignment**: Is the counterparty internally aligned or fragmented?
- **Precedent Constraints**: Are there templates, precedents, or policies that limit flexibility?
- **External Pressures**: Are there time pressures, competitive dynamics, or other external factors?
- **Relationship Quality**: Is there trust and good faith, or suspicion and adversarial posturing?
- **Experience Level**: Are both parties experienced and sophisticated, or will education be required?

**Complexity Scoring**:
- **Low complexity** (70-90% success rate): Standard deal, few contentious terms, aligned parties, good relationship
- **Moderate complexity** (40-70% success rate): Some difficult terms, moderate misalignment, workable relationship
- **High complexity** (15-40% success rate): Major gaps on multiple terms, poor relationship, significant internal misalignment
- **Extreme complexity** (<15% success rate): Fundamental disagreements, adversarial relationship, structural barriers

**Step 2.3: Calculate Probability-Adjusted Value**

Combine structural feasibility and negotiation complexity to estimate probability of success.

**Formula**:
- If structural barriers exist: Probability = 0-30%
- If no structural barriers: Probability = Complexity Score Range (midpoint)

**Expected Value** = Total Deal Value × Probability of Success

**Example**:
- Total deal value: $1.95M
- No structural barriers identified
- Moderate complexity (negotiation on pricing, liability caps, IP ownership)
- Estimated probability: 55%
- **Expected value: $1.07M**

### Phase 3: Resource Investment Assessment

**Step 3.1: Estimate Required Resources**

Calculate the full cost of pursuing the transaction.

Resource categories:
- **Legal Hours**: Attorney time (internal and external) at market rates
- **Business Development Time**: BD personnel time in meetings, negotiations, due diligence
- **Executive Time**: C-suite or senior management involvement
- **Technical Resources**: Engineering, IT, or product team time for evaluations or integrations
- **External Advisors**: Consultants, technical experts, accountants
- **Opportunity Cost**: What else could these resources be doing?

**Estimation Framework**:
- **Simple deal**: 20-40 hours legal, 10-20 hours business, minimal executive time
- **Standard deal**: 40-80 hours legal, 20-40 hours business, 5-10 hours executive
- **Complex deal**: 80-200 hours legal, 40-80 hours business, 10-30 hours executive
- **Highly complex deal**: 200+ hours legal, 80+ hours business, 30+ hours executive

**Cost Calculation**:
- External legal: $500-$1,000/hour
- Internal legal (opportunity cost): $300-$600/hour
- Business development: $200-$400/hour
- Executive time: $500-$2,000/hour

**Example**:
- Complex deal estimate: 120 hours legal (mix of internal/external), 60 hours business, 15 hours executive
- **Total cost: $120K**

**Step 3.2: Assess Opportunity Cost**

What alternatives would we forego by pursuing this deal?

Questions to ask:
- What other deals are in the pipeline?
- Which of those require similar resources (same attorneys, same BD personnel)?
- What is the expected value of those alternatives?
- Is there a timing conflict where pursuing this deal delays or prevents pursuing others?

**Opportunity Cost Calculation**:
If pursuing Deal A prevents pursuing Deal B:
- Opportunity Cost = Expected Value of Deal B

If pursuing Deal A delays but doesn't prevent Deal B:
- Opportunity Cost = (Time Value of Money on Deal B) + (Risk that delay causes Deal B to die)

**Step 3.3: Calculate Net Expected Value**

**Net Expected Value** = Expected Value - Resource Cost - Opportunity Cost

**Example**:
- Expected value: $1.07M
- Resource cost: $120K
- Opportunity cost: $200K (will delay pursuit of another opportunity)
- **Net expected value: $750K**

### Phase 4: Go/No-Go Decision Framework

**Step 4.1: Apply Decision Thresholds**

**Strong Go** (High Priority):
- Net expected value > $500K (or >3x resource cost)
- Strategic value rating: High
- Probability of success > 60%
- **Action**: Allocate top resources, invest heavily in negotiation

**Go** (Standard Priority):
- Net expected value > $200K (or >2x resource cost)
- Strategic value rating: Moderate to High
- Probability of success > 40%
- **Action**: Pursue with standard resource allocation

**Conditional Go** (Low Priority):
- Net expected value > $50K (or >1.5x resource cost)
- Strategic value rating: Low to Moderate
- Probability of success > 30%
- **Action**: Pursue only with minimal resource investment; accept worse terms rather than protracted negotiation

**No-Go** (Decline or Defer):
- Net expected value < $50K
- OR Probability of success < 30%
- OR Negative strategic value
- **Action**: Decline politely, or defer pending improved conditions

**Step 4.2: Apply Strategic Filters**

Even if financial metrics support proceeding, apply strategic screens:

**Strategic Misalignment**:
- Does this deal distract from core business?
- Does it create dependencies on problematic partners?
- Does it set bad precedents for future deals?
- **If yes to any**: Increase decision threshold (may decline even if financially attractive)

**Strategic Imperative**:
- Is this deal critical to company strategy regardless of direct economics?
- Is declining this deal more harmful than the resource cost?
- Does this deal create must-have options or capabilities?
- **If yes to any**: May proceed even if below standard threshold

**Step 4.3: Determine Resource Allocation Level**

For deals that pass the go/no-go threshold, calibrate resource investment:

**Maximum Investment** (Strategic Priority Deals):
- Allocate best attorneys and BD personnel
- Involve executives early and throughout
- Invest in building relationship
- Fight hard for optimal terms on all material issues
- Accept higher cost to maximize probability of success

**Standard Investment** (Normal Priority Deals):
- Allocate competent (not necessarily top) resources
- Involve executives selectively on key decisions
- Focus negotiation effort on highest-impact terms
- Accept standard market terms on secondary issues

**Minimal Investment** (Low Priority Deals):
- Allocate junior attorneys with senior oversight
- Minimize executive involvement
- Accept counterparty's standard terms on most issues
- Focus only on deal-breaker risks
- Set strict time limits on negotiation

### Phase 5: Ongoing Re-evaluation

**Step 5.1: Set Re-evaluation Triggers**

Establish conditions that would prompt reassessing the go/no-go decision.

Triggers:
- **Time elapsed**: If negotiation exceeds X weeks, reassess
- **Cost incurred**: If costs exceed Y% of estimate, reassess
- **Probability change**: If new information suggests deal is less likely to close, reassess
- **Competitive threat**: If pursuing this deal allows competitor to close a better opportunity, reassess
- **Strategic shift**: If company strategy changes, reassess deal alignment

**Step 5.2: Monitor Progress and Adjust**

Track actual resource consumption and probability updates.

Monthly review:
- Resources consumed vs. budget
- Progress toward close vs. timeline
- Updated probability assessment based on negotiations
- Revised expected value calculation

**Decision Rule**:
If at any point Net Expected Value < $0 or probability drops below no-go threshold:
- Escalate decision to walk away or dramatically reduce investment

**Step 5.3: Exit Criteria**

Define conditions under which you would walk away mid-negotiation.

Walk-away triggers:
- Resource cost exceeds expected value
- Probability drops below 20% due to insurmountable obstacles
- Counterparty negotiating in bad faith (using negotiation to extract competitive intelligence, delaying to benefit competitor, etc.)
- Better alternative opportunity emerges
- Strategic context changes making deal no longer valuable

## 4. Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

### Pitfall 1: Sunk Cost Fallacy

**Mistake**: Continuing to invest in a deal because you've already spent significant resources, even when updated analysis shows negative expected value.

**Example**: You've spent $80K in legal fees negotiating a complex partnership. The counterparty keeps adding new requirements and probability of close has dropped to 15%. You continue negotiating because "we've already invested so much."

**Warning Signs**:
- Justifying continued investment based on past spending
- Feeling you "can't walk away now after all this work"
- Ignoring updated probability estimates because you're "too far in"
- Not reassessing the go/no-go decision as circumstances change

**Remediation**:
- Apply Phase 5 re-evaluation triggers religiously
- Make decisions based on forward-looking expected value, not past costs
- Treat sunk costs as truly sunk - they're gone regardless of what you do next
- Establish clear walk-away criteria at the outset and enforce them
- Remember: Throwing good money after bad compounds the loss

**Decision Rule**: At any point, ask: "If we hadn't spent anything yet, would we start this negotiation now with current knowledge?" If no, walk away.

### Pitfall 2: Ignoring Opportunity Cost

**Mistake**: Evaluating deals in isolation without considering what else you could be doing with those resources.

**Example**: You pursue a $300K deal because it has positive expected value, without realizing that your top attorney's time on this deal prevents closing a $2M strategic partnership.

**Warning Signs**:
- Saying yes to every deal with positive expected value
- Not tracking what opportunities are being delayed or foregone
- Resource constraints becoming chronic (always behind, always busy)
- Missing strategic opportunities because resources are tied up in tactical deals

**Remediation**:
- Maintain a pipeline view of all opportunities and their resource requirements
- Explicitly calculate opportunity cost for each deal (Phase 3, Step 3.2)
- Prioritize deals based on net expected value (after opportunity cost)
- Use portfolio management: allocate fixed resources to highest-value opportunities
- Be willing to decline positive-EV deals if better opportunities exist

### Pitfall 3: Overweighting Direct Economic Value

**Mistake**: Focusing solely on contract value without considering strategic factors.

**Example**: Declining a $200K deal with a prestigious customer that would unlock $5M in similar deals, because the direct economics don't justify the $50K legal cost.

**Warning Signs**:
- Making decisions based purely on contract value
- Not having a framework for quantifying strategic value
- Treating all deals in the same category as equivalent
- Underinvesting in deals with high strategic importance

**Remediation**:
- Always apply strategic value assessment (Phase 1, Step 1.2)
- Use strategic multipliers to quantify intangible value
- Explicitly identify reference value, market access, competitive positioning benefits
- Consider option value - what future opportunities does this create?
- Distinguish between "profitable revenue" and "strategically valuable revenue"

### Pitfall 4: Overestimating Probability of Success

**Mistake**: Being overly optimistic about the likelihood of closing, especially for deals you're excited about.

**Example**: Assuming an 80% probability of close for a deal with major unresolved issues, fragmented decision-making on the counterparty side, and a 6-month sales cycle.

**Warning Signs**:
- Probability estimates consistently above 70% for complex deals
- Not distinguishing between structural feasibility and negotiation complexity
- Ignoring warning signs (scope creep, ghosting, delays, new decision-makers)
- Being surprised when deals fall through

**Remediation**:
- Use structured probability assessment (Phase 2, Steps 2.1-2.2)
- Calibrate against historical data: what % of similar deals actually closed?
- Identify structural barriers explicitly and adjust probability accordingly
- Get input from business development team on realistic close rates
- Update probability estimates as negotiations progress
- Apply red team analysis: what could cause this deal to fail?

### Pitfall 5: Uniform Resource Allocation

**Mistake**: Investing the same level of resources in all deals regardless of strategic importance or probability of success.

**Example**: Spending 100 hours negotiating aggressive terms for a low-value, low-probability deal because "we always fight for the best terms."

**Warning Signs**:
- Same level of attorney involvement on all deals
- Same level of negotiation intensity on all terms
- Not distinguishing between strategic priority deals and tactical deals
- Chronic resource constraints because every deal gets full treatment

**Remediation**:
- Apply resource allocation framework (Phase 4, Step 4.3)
- Explicitly tier deals: Maximum Investment vs. Standard vs. Minimal
- On low-priority deals, accept market-standard terms quickly
- Reserve aggressive negotiation for strategic priority deals
- Use templates and playbooks for standard deals to minimize resource consumption
- Train team to calibrate effort to deal importance

### Pitfall 6: Ignoring Walk-Away Options

**Mistake**: Feeling compelled to close every deal once negotiations start, even when circumstances change dramatically.

**Example**: Counterparty keeps adding requirements, timeline has stretched from 2 months to 8 months, cost has tripled, but you keep going because "we've come this far."

**Warning Signs**:
- Never walking away from deals mid-negotiation
- Feeling that walking away is "failure"
- Not having defined walk-away criteria
- Negotiating long past initial time/cost estimates without reassessing

**Remediation**:
- Establish clear exit criteria at the outset (Phase 5, Step 5.3)
- Empower attorneys to recommend walking away when criteria are met
- Reframe walking away as "resource reallocation" not "failure"
- Conduct regular reviews (monthly for long negotiations)
- Remember: Walking away from a bad deal is often the best decision

### Pitfall 7: Analysis Paralysis

**Mistake**: Spending so much time on deal qualification analysis that you miss the opportunity or consume resources that should go to execution.

**Example**: Conducting a 40-hour analysis to decide whether to pursue a straightforward $100K deal.

**Warning Signs**:
- Qualification analysis taking weeks for standard deals
- Missing deal opportunities due to slow decision-making
- Over-analyzing low-value, low-complexity transactions
- Requesting extensive data before making initial go/no-go decision

**Remediation**:
- Use quick screening for standard deals (30 minutes or less)
- Reserve deep analysis for complex, high-value, or uncertain situations
- Create deal tiers with corresponding analysis depth:
  - **Tier 1** (>$1M, high complexity): Full Phase 1-4 analysis
  - **Tier 2** ($250K-$1M, moderate complexity): Abbreviated analysis (key factors only)
  - **Tier 3** (<$250K, low complexity): Quick screen (15-30 minutes)
- Make initial go/no-go decisions with limited information, refine as negotiations progress
- Remember: The cost of analysis is part of resource investment

## 5. Integration with Other Patterns

### BI1 + S1: Stakeholder-Driven Deal Qualification

**Integration**: Use stakeholder analysis to inform probability of success and strategic value.

**Application**:
- Map stakeholders (S1) to identify who must approve the deal
- Assess their interests and power to determine probability of alignment
- Evaluate whether key stakeholders have positive expected value
- Identify stakeholder-related strategic value (key relationships, market access via partner)

**Example**: SaaS deal where stakeholder analysis reveals fragmented decision-making (IT wants to buy, finance has budget concerns, legal is risk-averse, procurement wants competitive bids). This substantially lowers probability of success and should inform go/no-go decision.

### BI1 + S4: Risk-Adjusted Deal Qualification

**Integration**: Use systematic risk assessment to adjust both probability and strategic value.

**Application**:
- Identify major risks (S4) and assess probability of risk materialization
- Determine if risks are dealbreakers (structural barriers) or negotiable (affect probability)
- Adjust expected value for risks that can't be fully mitigated
- Include cost of risk mitigation in resource investment calculation

**Example**: Data partnership with significant privacy risk. Risk assessment (S4) reveals 30% probability of regulatory challenge. This reduces expected value by 30% and must be factored into qualification decision.

### BI1 + S8: Scenario-Based Deal Valuation

**Integration**: Use scenario planning to refine expected value calculations.

**Application**:
- Generate scenarios for how the deal might unfold (S8)
- Assign probabilities to each scenario
- Calculate value in each scenario
- Compute probability-weighted expected value across scenarios

**Example**: Technology partnership with three scenarios:
- **Scenario A (40% probability)**: Successful collaboration, market adoption, $10M value
- **Scenario B (35% probability)**: Technical challenges, limited market, $1M value
- **Scenario C (25% probability)**: Failed integration, deal terminates, -$500K value
- **Expected value**: (0.4 × $10M) + (0.35 × $1M) + (0.25 × -$500K) = $4.225M

### BI1 + BI2: Qualification Drives Incentive Design

**Integration**: Go/no-go decision informs how much creativity to invest in incentive design.

**Application**:
- If deal is Strong Go (high priority): Invest heavily in creative economic structures (BI2) to maximize probability
- If deal is Conditional Go (low priority): Accept standard terms, don't invest in custom incentive design
- If structural barriers exist: Use BI2 to explore if creative economic structures can overcome them

**Example**: Strategic partnership initially scores as No-Go due to misaligned economic interests. However, creative incentive design (BI2) using milestone payments, revenue sharing, and earn-outs creates alignment. This converts to Conditional Go pending successful pilot.

### BI1 + BI3: Risk Calibration for Resource Allocation

**Integration**: Context-aware risk assessment determines appropriate resource investment.

**Application**:
- For strategic priority deals: Accept higher risks and fight harder for optimal terms
- For low priority deals: Accept worse risk allocation to minimize negotiation time
- Calibrate resource investment to both deal value and company context (runway, stage, strategic position)

**Example**: Early-stage startup with 8 months runway. BI1 analysis shows deal is moderately valuable but will take 4 months to negotiate. BI3 context (short runway) leads to decision: Accept worse terms to close in 4 weeks, or walk away to focus on faster deals.

### BI1 + BI4: Negotiation Capital Allocation

**Integration**: Deal qualification determines negotiation capital budget.

**Application**:
- Strong Go deals: High negotiation capital budget, fight hard on multiple fronts
- Standard Go deals: Moderate capital budget, focus on key terms
- Conditional Go deals: Minimal capital budget, accept worse terms quickly

**Example**: Portfolio of 5 active deals. BI1 qualification identifies 1 as strategic priority, 3 as standard, 1 as low priority. Allocate 50% of negotiation capital to strategic deal, 40% across the 3 standard deals, 10% to low-priority deal.
