# AP API Training-Based Profiling

## Extracting Top Performer Trait Profiles Through Professional Development

**Version 1.0 | January 2026** **Author: Kevin Keller, Tucuxi Inc.**

---

## Executive Summary

Traditional top performer profiling asks employees to participate in what feels like an interview for their own job—creating defensiveness and socially desirable responses. This document presents an alternative: **extracting authentic trait profiles through training content development sessions** where top performers are positioned as experts contributing to organizational learning rather than subjects being evaluated.

The key insight: **When people teach, they reveal their true mental models. When people are evaluated, they reveal their interview skills.**

---

## Part 1: The Psychology of the Reframe

### Why Traditional Profiling Creates Resistance

| Signal | Employee Interpretation | Resulting Behavior |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| "We want to interview our top performers" | "Am I being evaluated?" | Guarded responses |
| "Tell us what makes you successful" | "Are they documenting my secret sauce?" | Omission of key insights |
| "What traits should we hire for?" | "Are they planning to replace me?" | Generic, safe answers |
| Scheduled 1:1 with HR/consultant | "This feels like a performance review" | Socially desirable responses |

### Why Training Framing Creates Openness

| Signal | Employee Interpretation | Resulting Behavior |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| "We're building training for new hires" | "My expertise is valued" | Pride, thoroughness |
| "What would a new person get wrong?" | "I can share hard-won wisdom" | Authentic warnings |
| "Help us create realistic scenarios" | "I'm the expert here" | Detailed, specific examples |
| "What's the non-obvious thing?" | "Finally someone asks the real question" | Deep tacit knowledge |

### The Expert-Teacher Effect

When positioned as teachers, people:

- **Organize their knowledge** (revealing structure of expertise)  
- **Anticipate failure modes** (revealing what traits prevent failure)  
- **Share "obvious" things** (revealing unconscious competence)  
- **Warn against shortcuts** (revealing values and standards)  
- **Tell stories** (revealing behavioral patterns)

## Part 2: Scenario Generation Framework

### 2.1 Scenario Components

Every role-specific scenario should include:

SCENARIO STRUCTURE:

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐

│ CONTEXT                                                      │

│ \- Role-appropriate setting                                   │

│ \- Realistic constraints (time, resources, information)       │

│ \- Stakeholder landscape                                      │

├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤

│ TRIGGER                                                      │

│ \- Specific event or request                                  │

│ \- Embedded ambiguity or tension                              │

│ \- Multiple valid paths forward                               │

├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤

│ DECISION POINT                                               │

│ \- What does the person need to do/decide?                    │

│ \- What information is missing?                               │

│ \- What competing priorities exist?                           │

├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤

│ TRAIT SURFACE AREA                                           │

│ \- Which traits does this scenario reveal?                    │

│ \- What distinguishes good vs. great responses?               │

└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

### 2.2 Scenario Types by Trait Surface

| Scenario Type | Primary Traits Surfaced | Example Pattern |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Ambiguous Request** | Curiosity, Communication | Incomplete information; requires clarification |
| **Competing Priorities** | Adaptability, Initiative | Two valid paths, limited resources |
| **Interpersonal Friction** | Collaboration, Communication | Stakeholder conflict or misalignment |
| **Unexpected Change** | Adaptability, Resilience | Plan disruption mid-execution |
| **Resource Constraint** | Initiative, Collaboration | Not enough time/people/budget |
| **Quality vs. Speed** | Initiative, Resilience | Pressure to cut corners |
| **Scope Creep** | Communication, Adaptability | Requirements shifting during work |
| **Knowledge Gap** | Curiosity, Communication | Need to learn something quickly |
| **Failure Recovery** | Resilience, Collaboration | Something went wrong, now what? |
| **Success Attribution** | Collaboration | Project succeeded, who gets credit? |

### 2.3 Generating Scenarios from Job Descriptions

**Input**: Job description text **Process**:

1\. EXTRACT KEY RESPONSIBILITIES

   \- What are the 5-7 core activities?

   \- What decisions does this role make?

   \- Who does this role interact with?

2\. IDENTIFY DECISION POINTS

   \- Where does judgment matter?

   \- What can go wrong?

   \- Where do paths diverge?

3\. ADD REALISTIC CONSTRAINTS

   \- Time pressure

   \- Information gaps

   \- Stakeholder complexity

   \- Resource limitations

4\. EMBED TRAIT-REVEALING TENSIONS

   \- Speed vs. quality

   \- Individual vs. team

   \- Short-term vs. long-term

   \- Process vs. outcome

5\. CREATE SCENARIO SET

   \- 2-3 scenarios per trait \= 12-18 total

   \- Vary difficulty (routine, challenging, crisis)

   \- Include both individual and collaborative situations

## Part 3: Role-Specific Scenario Libraries

### 3.1 ADMINISTRATIVE: Front Desk / Receptionist

**Role Essence**: First point of contact, traffic controller, problem solver, tone-setter

#### Scenario 1: The Demanding VIP (Adaptability, Communication)

**Context**: You're at the front desk on a busy Monday morning. The phone is ringing, two delivery people are waiting, and three employees need badge replacements.

**Trigger**: A person walks in and says they have a meeting with the CEO in five minutes but they're not on the visitor list. They're clearly agitated and say, "She knows I'm coming—we set this up yesterday."

**Decision Point**: How do you handle this while managing everything else?

**Extraction Questions**:

- "Walk me through how you'd approach this. What's your first move?"  
- "What would a new receptionist typically do wrong here?"  
- "What's the non-obvious thing that matters in this situation?"  
- "How do you read whether this person is legitimate vs. problematic?"

**Trait Mapping**: | Response Pattern | Indicates | |-----------------|-----------| | Prioritizes the agitated person immediately | May struggle with competing demands | | Creates a quick system (triage) | Strong adaptability | | Tone-matches while gathering info | Strong communication | | Calls upstairs immediately without checking | May lack appropriate skepticism | | Asks clarifying questions to the visitor | Curiosity \+ communication |

#### Scenario 2: The Unreasonable Request (Initiative, Resilience)

**Context**: You've been at the front desk for three months. The office supply system is a mess—people constantly can't find things, and you spend 20% of your time helping locate supplies.

**Trigger**: Your manager mentions in passing, "The supply situation is kind of chaos, huh?" and walks away.

**Decision Point**: What, if anything, do you do?

**Extraction Questions**:

- "What would you do with that comment?"  
- "If you decided to fix it, how would you approach it?"  
- "What would stop a new person from taking this on?"  
- "How do you know when something is your problem to solve vs. not?"

#### Scenario 3: The Difficult Regular (Collaboration, Resilience)

**Context**: There's an employee who comes to the front desk multiple times per day with complaints—the coffee is cold, the bathroom is dirty, someone parked in their spot. They're never satisfied with your answers.

**Trigger**: Today they're back, upset that their package hasn't arrived yet, and they're implying you lost it.

**Decision Point**: How do you handle this interaction and this pattern?

**Extraction Questions**:

- "How do you handle this specific interaction?"  
- "How do you handle this pattern over time?"  
- "What would a new person get wrong here?"  
- "When, if ever, do you escalate someone like this?"

### 3.2 LEGAL: Product Counsel

**Role Essence**: Risk translator, business partner, technical learner, creative problem-solver

#### Scenario 1: The Last-Minute Launch (Adaptability, Communication)

**Context**: A product team has been working on a feature for six months. You've reviewed it twice and provided guidance. Launch is scheduled for tomorrow.

**Trigger**: At 4 PM, the PM pings you: "Hey, quick question—we added a small thing where users can upload photos. Same review applies, right?"

**Decision Point**: A "small thing" that introduces significant new legal considerations. Launch is in 18 hours.

**Extraction Questions**:

- "Walk me through your first 30 minutes after getting this message."  
- "How do you communicate the problem without becoming the 'blocker'?"  
- "What's the non-obvious risk a junior counsel might miss?"  
- "How do you handle the PM's frustration if you need to delay?"

**Trait Mapping**: | Response Pattern | Indicates | |-----------------|-----------| | Immediately calls to understand scope | Curiosity \+ urgency awareness | | Provides options, not just "no" | Strong collaboration | | Documents the risk for the record | Initiative, appropriate CYA | | Panics or immediately escalates to GC | May lack adaptability | | Asks what's the business impact of delay | Strategic thinking |

#### Scenario 2: The Technical Deep Dive (Curiosity, Communication)

**Context**: You're supporting a new AI product team. The engineers keep using terms you don't fully understand—"embeddings," "fine-tuning," "hallucinations."

**Trigger**: The engineering lead asks you to review their model card for regulatory compliance. You understand maybe 40% of it.

**Decision Point**: How do you get up to speed while maintaining credibility?

**Extraction Questions**:

- "How do you approach learning this?"  
- "What questions would you ask, and who would you ask them to?"  
- "How do you provide useful legal guidance when you don't fully understand the technology?"  
- "What would a new product counsel get wrong here?"

#### Scenario 3: The Overruled Advice (Resilience, Collaboration)

**Context**: You've advised strongly against a particular data sharing arrangement. You documented your concerns. The business leadership decided to proceed anyway, with sign-off from the GC.

**Trigger**: Six months later, exactly what you predicted happens—a vendor misuses the data, and there's a PR crisis. The CEO asks in a meeting, "How did we end up here?"

**Decision Point**: How do you handle this moment?

**Extraction Questions**:

- "What do you say in that meeting?"  
- "How do you handle the temptation to say 'I told you so'?"  
- "What's the right way to have documented this originally?"  
- "How does this experience affect your approach going forward?"

### 3.3 ENGINEERING: Software Developer

**Role Essence**: Problem solver, system thinker, collaborator, continuous learner

#### Scenario 1: The Inherited Codebase (Curiosity, Adaptability)

**Context**: You've joined a team that owns a critical service. The original authors have all left. The code has minimal documentation and some... creative architectural choices.

**Trigger**: Your first task is to add a "simple" feature. As you dig in, you realize the system is more fragile than anyone knew.

**Decision Point**: How do you approach this feature while managing what you've discovered?

**Extraction Questions**:

- "Walk me through your first few days with this codebase."  
- "How do you decide what to investigate vs. leave alone?"  
- "When and how do you raise the alarm about technical debt?"  
- "What would a new engineer typically do wrong here?"

**Trait Mapping**: | Response Pattern | Indicates | |-----------------|-----------| | Just ships the feature, moves on | May lack curiosity or ownership | | Creates a full refactoring plan before doing anything | May lack pragmatism | | Documents findings while shipping feature | Strong initiative \+ adaptability | | Immediately escalates to manager | May lack resilience | | Writes tests to understand behavior | Strong curiosity \+ engineering judgment |

#### Scenario 2: The Disagreement on Approach (Collaboration, Communication)

**Context**: You're designing a solution with a senior engineer. You believe microservices is the right approach; they strongly prefer a monolith. You have good reasons; so do they.

**Trigger**: The tech lead asks you both to present your recommendations to the team tomorrow.

**Decision Point**: How do you prepare and present when you genuinely disagree with a more senior colleague?

**Extraction Questions**:

- "How do you prepare for this presentation?"  
- "How do you present your view while respecting theirs?"  
- "What if you lose the argument—how do you proceed?"  
- "What would a new engineer get wrong in this situation?"

#### Scenario 3: The On-Call Crisis (Resilience, Initiative)

**Context**: It's 2 AM. You're on call. Pager goes off—the main service is down. You're the only engineer awake.

**Trigger**: You look at the error logs and see something you've never encountered before. The runbook doesn't cover this case. Users are impacted NOW.

**Decision Point**: What do you do?

**Extraction Questions**:

- "Walk me through your first 10 minutes."  
- "When do you wake someone else up?"  
- "How do you balance 'fix it fast' vs. 'understand what's happening'?"  
- "What would a new engineer typically do wrong here?"

#### Scenario 2: The Unrealistic Promise (Communication, Resilience)

**Context**: You're in a competitive deal. The prospect says, "Your competitor promised they can implement in 30 days. Can you match that?"

**Trigger**: You know your implementation typically takes 60-90 days. Your competitor is probably lying.

**Decision Point**: How do you respond?

**Extraction Questions**:

- "What would a new AE get wrong here?"

**Trigger**: Your quota is tight this quarter. This deal would put you over.

**Decision Point**: What do you do?

**Extraction Questions**:

- "How do you balance quota pressure with long-term thinking?"  
- "What would a new AE typically do wrong here?"

## Part 4: Extraction Question Framework

### 4.1 Core Question Types

| Question Type | Purpose | Trait Surface |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **"Walk me through..."** | Reveals process and priorities | Adaptability, Initiative |
| **"What would someone new get wrong?"** | Surfaces non-obvious insights | All (expert teaching mode) |
| **"What's the non-obvious thing?"** | Accesses tacit knowledge | Curiosity, Experience depth |
| **"When would this NOT apply?"** | Tests nuance and adaptability | Adaptability |
| **"How do you know when to..."** | Reveals decision frameworks | All metacognitive traits |
| **"What would stop someone from..."** | Identifies failure modes | Resilience, Initiative |
| **"How do you balance..."** | Reveals priority structures | Adaptability, Communication |
| **"Have you ever actually..."** | Gets real behavioral data | All (verification mode) |

### 4.2 Universal Extraction Sequences

**Sequence A: Teaching Mode Activation**

1. "We're building training for this exact situation. How would you explain the right approach?"  
2. "What's the trap that looks right but isn't?"  
3. "What would you wish someone had told you earlier in your career?"

**Sequence B: Failure Pattern Mining**

1. "Where do people typically struggle with this?"  
2. "What's a mistake you made early on that taught you something?"  
3. "How do you spot someone who's about to make that mistake?"

**Sequence C: Expert Intuition Extraction**

1. "How do you read this situation—what signals do you look for?"  
2. "What do you notice that less experienced people miss?"  
3. "When do your instincts override the 'right' process?"

**Sequence D: Trait-Specific Deep Dive**

1. "What personal quality makes you good at this?" (Direct trait inquiry)  
2. "When is that quality a liability?" (Nuance check)  
3. "How did you develop that?" (Trainability insight)

### 4.3 Response-to-Trait Mapping

**When analyzing responses, look for**:

| Response Contains | Likely Indicates | Trait Signal |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Multiple options considered | Strong solution space thinking | ADAPTABILITY, CURIOSITY |
| Stakeholder awareness | Strong perspective-taking | COLLABORATION, COMMUNICATION |
| Process \+ exceptions | Nuanced judgment | ADAPTABILITY |
| Unprompted warnings | Pattern recognition | RESILIENCE (learned from failure) |
| Questions back at you | Active engagement | CURIOSITY |
| "It depends on..." | Contextual thinking | ADAPTABILITY |
| Stories with failure | Psychological safety | RESILIENCE |
| Credit to others | Healthy ego | COLLABORATION |
| "I would..." vs "You should..." | Ownership vs. advice-giving | INITIATIVE |

## Part 5: Session Design

### 5.1 Framing Script

**Invitation**:

"We're developing training materials for new hires in \[role\]. As someone who's been consistently excellent in this position, we'd love your input on realistic scenarios and how to handle them. This will help us onboard people faster and set them up for success. Would you be willing to spend 45 minutes helping us build this?"

**Session Opening**:

"Thank you for doing this. Your expertise is exactly what we need to create something useful. I'm going to walk through some scenarios that new \[role\] might face, and I want to hear how you'd approach them and what advice you'd give. There are no wrong answers—I'm trying to capture how excellent people actually think about this work."

### 5.2 Session Structure

| Time | Phase | Content |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 0-5 min | **Setup** | Thank them, explain purpose, establish expert positioning |
| 5-15 min | **Warm-up Scenario** | Easy scenario, full extraction sequence |
| 15-35 min | **Core Scenarios** | 3-4 trait-targeted scenarios with extraction |
| 35-40 min | **Meta Questions** | "What makes someone great at this role?" |
| 40-45 min | **Close** | Thank them, explain how input will be used |

### 5.3 Meta-Questions for End of Session

- "If you could only hire for one trait, what would it be?"  
- "What's the trait that people think matters but actually doesn't?"  
- "What surprised you about what makes someone successful here?"  
- "What type of person consistently struggles in this role?"  
- "What's something you've developed specifically for this job?"

## Part 7: Additional Benefits

### 7.1 Dual Output Value

This approach produces **two valuable outputs** from one session:

| Output | Value | Recipient |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Trait Profile | Candidate screening parameters | Hiring team |
| Training Content | Actual onboarding materials | New hires |

### 7.2 Engagement & Retention Signal

The **way** top performers engage with this process reveals retention-relevant data:

| Engagement Pattern | May Indicate |
| :---- | :---- |
| Enthusiastic, detailed responses | High engagement, feels valued |
| Rushed, generic responses | May be checked out |
| "I've been wanting to document this" | Succession planning mindset |
| "No one ever asks me this" | May feel undervalued |
| Suggests improvements to the process | High ownership, initiative |

### 7.3 Organizational Learning

Repeated profiling across roles reveals:

- Which traits are **organization-wide** vs. **role-specific**  
- Where training gaps exist (if top performers struggle to articulate)  
- Cultural values embedded in "how we do things here"  
- Potential single points of failure (knowledge held by few)
